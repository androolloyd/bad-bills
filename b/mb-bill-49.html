Bill 49RIGHTS-POSITIVE

The Business Practices Amendment Act (algorithmic / personalized pricing)

Sponsor: Hon. Mintu Sandhu, Minister of Public Service Delivery (NDP) · Status: Royal Assent June 1, 2026; marked 'P' — passed, awaiting proclamation. · Manitoba · Provincial

Regulates 'personalized algorithmic pricing' — using algorithms and large-scale personal-data collection (behaviour, demographics, location, financial status) to charge a specific consumer more. Prohibits online retailers/distributors from using algorithmic pricing to raise prices, bans surprise higher prices via electronic shelf labels without clear consumer consent, requires prominent disclosure of price and fees, and gives consumers access to the data used about them.

✅ The case for

An early, notable Canadian move against opaque 'surveillance pricing.' Privacy scholar Teresa Scassa flags that algorithmic price discrimination is normally invisible to consumers, so a ban plus a data-access right is a meaningful consumer-privacy protection. Frames covert data-driven price discrimination as an unfair business practice.

⚠️ The case against

Enforceability is uncertain — detecting hidden algorithmic pricing is difficult, and definitions/consent mechanics may be hard to police. Scope (provincial, consumer-goods focused) limits reach; effectiveness depends on how aggressively it is enforced and on forthcoming details.

🔐 Privacy & civil liberties: Strongly privacy-positive: directly targets exploitation of personal data for individualized pricing and creates a consumer right to see the data used to price them. Among the most pro-privacy measures of the session.

👥 Who it affects

All Manitoba consumers (especially online shoppers); online retailers, distributors and any business deploying dynamic/personalized pricing or electronic shelf labels.

✔️ Fact-checkVerified: Bill 49, The Business Practices Amendment Act (Minister Sandhu), passed Standing Committee unamended on April 22, 2026 and received royal assent June 1, 2026 (SM 2026, c. 41); detailed rules will follow in regulation. It makes it an unfair business practice for retailers and online marketplaces to use 'personalized algorithmic pricing' (dynamic pricing based on a specific consumer's data) to INCREASE the price charged to that consumer, with corporate fines up to $300,000 (first offence) and $1,000,000 (subsequent). It would make Manitoba the first Canadian jurisdiction to legislate on personalized algorithmic pricing. Note: it targets price increases to individuals, not ordinary discounts or uniform sales.
