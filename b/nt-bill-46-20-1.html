Bill 46-20(1)RIGHTS-POSITIVE

Prevention of Proceedings That Hamper Expression on Matters of Public Interest Act (anti-SLAPP)

Sponsor: George Nerysoo, MLA for Mackenzie Delta (Private Member's bill, introduced at the request of Tsiigehtchic Elder Lawrence Norbert) · Status: Introduced and passed second reading March 2026; under review by the Standing Committee on Government Operations. Awaiting committee report, third reading and Assent (not yet passed as of mid-June 2026). If enacted, it would be the first anti-SLAPP law in any Canadian territory. · Northwest Territories · Provincial

This is anti-SLAPP legislation (SLAPP = Strategic Lawsuit Against Public Participation). It is designed to protect people who speak out on matters of public interest from being silenced by meritless or intimidating lawsuits. It creates a fast-track court process: a defendant sued over public-interest expression can apply for early dismissal, and a court may throw out the suit unless the plaintiff shows the claim has substantial merit, the defendant has no valid defence, and the harm from the expression outweighs the public interest in protecting it. Successful defendants can recover costs and, where a suit was brought in bad faith, damages. It does not shield deliberately false, hateful or malicious speech. It is modelled on laws in Ontario, British Columbia, Quebec and Manitoba.

✅ The case for

Rights-expanding: strengthens freedom of expression and public participation (Charter s. 2(b)) by deterring 'libel-chill' lawsuits used by powerful interests to silence critics, journalists, activists and community members; provides a quick, lower-cost off-ramp so defendants are not financially crushed; allows cost and bad-faith damage recovery; would make the NWT a Canadian leader as the first territory with such a law; arose from grassroots advocacy by a Gwich'in Elder.

⚠️ The case against

Critics of anti-SLAPP regimes generally caution that an overbroad dismissal mechanism could be misused to short-circuit legitimate defamation claims and deny plaintiffs (including ordinary people whose reputations are genuinely harmed) their day in court; effectiveness depends on courts and litigants understanding a new and fairly technical procedure; the carve-outs for false/hateful/malicious speech will require careful judicial line-drawing.

🔐 Privacy & civil liberties: Minimal / positive for civil liberties. The bill protects expression and reputation-related litigants rather than collecting personal data; its main effect is to enhance free-expression and access-to-justice rights, with no new surveillance or data-collection powers.

👥 Who it affects

Journalists, activists, whistleblowers, community members and commenters who speak on public-interest matters; people and organizations who bring (or face) defamation/other suits arising from such speech; the courts.
