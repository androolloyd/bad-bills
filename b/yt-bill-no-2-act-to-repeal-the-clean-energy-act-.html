Act to repeal the Clean Energy Act

Sponsor: Ted Laking, Minister of Energy, Mines and Resources (Yukon Party) · Status: Assented to. Introduced March 24, 2026; passed third reading 12-6 on March 30, 2026 and received assent. EV/e-bike rebate programs were cancelled and ~$946,000 redirected to home-heating (dual-fuel) incentives and electricity-bill relief. · Yukon · Provincial

Repeals Yukon's 2022 Clean Energy Act, eliminating the territory's legislated greenhouse-gas reduction targets (including net-zero by 2050), its electric-vehicle mandate, and the requirement to publicly report annual emissions and progress. The government redirects rebate funding away from EVs, EV chargers and e-bikes (previously up to $5,000/EV and $1,500/e-bike) toward incentives for non-electric (dual-fuel) home heating, arguing this reduces peak winter electricity demand after a December 2025 cold snap brought the grid close to rolling blackouts.

✅ The case for

Government argues repeal is needed to cut peak winter electricity load and protect grid reliability after December's near-blackout, and that targets such as net-zero by 2050 were unachievable and were themselves driving up electricity demand. Frees up funds for immediate electricity-bill relief and dual-fuel heating that eases grid strain during cold snaps. Buys time to develop a 'winter energy plan.'

⚠️ The case against

Repeals legislated emissions targets AND the legal accountability/reporting mechanism, which experts say removes transparency on whether Yukon is meeting climate goals. Yukon University climate researcher Brian Horton called eliminating the targets discouraging and a setback for accountability. The Council of Yukon First Nations said it was not consulted and warned the move undermines years of joint climate/energy work and self-government partnership; First Nations called it backsliding. Critics note subsidizing fossil-fuel heating runs counter to climate commitments. Opposition voted against it 6-12.

🔐 Privacy & civil liberties: Minimal. The repeal concerns energy/climate policy, rebates and emissions reporting; it contains no personal-data collection or surveillance provisions.

👥 Who it affects

All Yukon ratepayers and the electrical grid; prospective EV and e-bike buyers (rebates removed); homeowners (new dual-fuel heating incentives); Yukon First Nations (consultation and co-governance concerns); and the territory's climate-accountability framework.
