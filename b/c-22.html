C-22HIGH CONCERN

Lawful Access Act, 2026

Sponsor: Public Safety Minister Gary Anandasangaree · Status: In committee (SECU) — passed 2nd reading Apr 20; being rushed before summer · Federal · Parliament of Canada

The surveillance powers carved out of the border bill (C-2). Lets police and CSIS demand "subscriber information" (name, address, account and device IDs) on a low "reasonable suspicion" threshold without a warrant; adds "confirmation of service" demands with gag orders; metadata retention; and pressures providers to build access capabilities.

✅ The case for

Police say the law predates modern apps and slows serious investigations.

⚠️ The case against

The CCLA and Citizen Lab catalogued its flaws; the Privacy Commissioner, Michael Geist and Fasken call it the central privacy concern of the session. Apple warned it would force companies to break encryption by inserting backdoors.

🔐 Privacy & civil liberties: The country's highest-stakes privacy fight: warrantless/low-threshold data access, gag orders, encryption backdoors, and U.S. cross-border data demands (CLOUD Act).

👥 Who it affects

Everyone with a phone or internet account; encrypted-service users; journalists and their sources.

✔️ Fact-checkVerified: Bill C-22 (Lawful Access Act, 2026), sponsored by the Minister of Public Safety, is at committee study in the House of Commons (second reading and referral to committee on April 20, 2026); it is NOT yet law. It is the standalone successor to the lawful-access provisions (former Parts 14 and 15) stripped out of Bill C-2 in October 2025; its Part 1 narrows the scope of warrantless information requests compared to C-2, but critics (Citizen Lab, Apple, CCLA, Michael Geist) warn its technical-capability mandate could still compel encryption backdoors and require up to one year of metadata retention.
