Bill 34-20(1)WATCH CLOSELY

Trespass to Property Act

Sponsor: Hon. Jay Macdonald, Minister of Justice (Government bill) · Status: Passed — all 18 voting MLAs supported it; received Assent March 6, 2026 following January 2026 public hearings in Yellowknife and Hay River and nine committee amendments. The NWT's first stand-alone trespass statute. · Northwest Territories · Provincial

Bill 34 creates the NWT's first general trespass law, making it an offence to enter or remain on premises without permission (or after being told to leave). The government presents it as part of its public-safety package — helping businesses and property owners remove unwanted or disruptive individuals and giving police clearer authority to deal with, and arrest, drug dealers operating on properties where they are not welcome. After committee review the bill was amended nine times and given explicit safeguards so that Indigenous and treaty rights take precedence over the Act in the event of conflict.

✅ The case for

Fills a real gap (the NWT previously had no general trespass statute); gives property owners and businesses a clear legal tool against unwanted entry and harassment; supports the broader anti-drug/public-safety effort; importantly includes explicit recognition that Indigenous and treaty rights (e.g., harvesting, access) prevail over the Act; passed unanimously after extensive public hearings and amendments, reflecting consensus refinement.

⚠️ The case against

Expanding who can enforce trespass and detain people raised civil-liberties concerns: MLA Shauna Morgan questioned whether bylaw officers or private security — not just RCMP — should be able to detain someone merely for being on a property without causing any other trouble; a committee urged the government to urgently regulate private security firms, noting security guards had been charged with assaulting an Elder; risk of disproportionate enforcement against homeless or Indigenous people in public-facing spaces.

🔐 Privacy & civil liberties: Low-to-medium. The Act itself is about physical access rather than data, but broader enforcement/detention powers (especially if exercised by private security or bylaw officers) raise concerns about arbitrary detention and identification of individuals; the committee's call to regulate private security speaks to oversight gaps.

👥 Who it affects

Property owners and businesses; people present on others' property (including homeless individuals and customers asked to leave); RCMP, bylaw officers and private security; Indigenous harvesters and rights-holders (whose treaty rights are expressly protected).
