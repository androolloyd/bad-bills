Bill 42LOWER CONCERN

The Saskatchewan Firearms Amendment Act, 2025

Sponsor: Hon. Tim McLeod (Minister of Justice / Attorney General; Minister responsible for the Saskatchewan Firearms Office), Sask Party · Status: Royal Assent March 12, 2026; in force by Order in Council - now law and operative. · Saskatchewan · Provincial

A provincial pushback against the federal firearms buyback. Requires that anyone (i.e., the federal government) who 'seizes' a firearm under federal law must pay the owner fair market value as set by the Saskatchewan Firearms Commissioner; firearms are deemed 'seized' if affected by a federal ban and the owner is not fairly compensated within 12 months. Sets up an online appraisal portal for affected firearms/ammunition/accessories (to create court evidence), and (via a late amendment) allows exemption certificates for affected lawful owners.

✅ The case for

Supported by firearms-owner groups (e.g., CSSA) and the government as protecting law-abiding Saskatchewan gun owners' property rights and ensuring fair compensation. Politically popular in much of rural Saskatchewan.

⚠️ The case against

Gun-control advocates argue it is a constitutionally questionable attempt to frustrate a valid federal public-safety program (firearms are federal jurisdiction), risks intergovernmental conflict and litigation, and could create administrative gridlock around the buyback. Effectiveness depends on whether provincial appraisal/exemption mechanisms can lawfully bind Ottawa.

🔐 Privacy & civil liberties: Low / mostly property-rights rather than privacy. The appraisal portal collects firearm-ownership data provincially, which has some data-handling implications, but it is largely a property-compensation and federal-provincial-jurisdiction measure rather than a surveillance one.

👥 Who it affects

Licensed firearms owners in Saskatchewan whose guns are caught by federal prohibitions, the federal buyback program, and the provincial Firearms Office.

✔️ Fact-checkVerified: Bill 42, The Saskatchewan Firearms Amendment Act, 2025, amends the existing Saskatchewan Firearms Act, was passed and received Royal Assent in 2026, and adds a 'deemed seizure' framework requiring fair-market compensation (within 12 months) before federally banned firearms are treated as seized, plus self-storage and provincial appraisal options for owners. Common misinformation correction: the law cannot block or override the federal firearms ban/buyback (criminal law and firearms regulation in this area are federal); it is largely a jurisdictional and political pushback measure imposing provincial conditions and compensation demands, and even supporters acknowledge parts may face a constitutional challenge.
