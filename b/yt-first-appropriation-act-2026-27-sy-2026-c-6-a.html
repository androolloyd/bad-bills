First Appropriation Act 2026-27 (Budget 2026)

Sponsor: Premier Currie Dixon (also Finance Minister), Yukon Party · Status: Assented to. Tabled March 19, 2026; passed on the final day of the spring sitting, May 11, 2026, after the first full line-by-line debate of the budget in 23 years (no 'guillotine' time-allocation clause used). An Interim Supply Appropriation Act (SY 2026, c.1) providing ~$556M of early spending authority was passed first. · Yukon · Provincial

Yukon's $2.46-billion operating and capital budget for fiscal year 2026-27: roughly $2.07 billion in operation-and-maintenance spending and $385 million in capital. It forecasts a record $81.8-million deficit and puts net financial debt around $804 million. Premier Dixon warned the territory has hit its federally imposed $1.2-billion borrowing cap and has asked Ottawa to raise it to $3 billion (matching the NWT). The budget frames itself as the 'first step on the road to fiscal recovery' after what the government calls unsustainable spending by the previous Liberal government.

✅ The case for

First full, line-by-line legislative scrutiny of a Yukon budget in 23 years, with every department vote debated and no guillotine clause - a transparency improvement praised across the chamber. Maintains core public services and signals fiscal restraint as the territory nears its debt ceiling. Pairs with a new $100M voted contingency (see Financial Administration Act) to handle wildfires and emergencies without repeated fall supplementary budgets.

⚠️ The case against

Record $81.8M deficit and a hit debt cap leave little fiscal room; opposition argued the budget does little to directly help residents with cost of living. NDP Leader Kate White asked 'How does this budget support people?' and criticized the 'irony' of the government pleading poverty while spending on conference travel and speechwriting. Liberal leader Debra-Leigh Reti said Yukon Party promises like rolling back electricity-rate increases may have to wait. Some campaign commitments appear deferred given the fiscal squeeze.

🔐 Privacy & civil liberties: Minimal. As an appropriation act it authorizes spending and contains no surveillance, data-collection, or civil-liberties provisions.

👥 Who it affects

All Yukoners - funds health care, education, housing, social services, infrastructure and First Nations programming; the debt-cap constraint affects the territory's future fiscal capacity.
