Bill 26RIGHTS-POSITIVE

An Act to Amend the Employment Standards Act (long-term illness and injury leave)

Sponsor: Hon. Jean-Claude D'Amours (Minister of Post-Secondary Education, Training and Labour; Liberal) · Status: Royal Assent / law (passed spring 2026; introduced March 2026). Verified on legnb.ca. · New Brunswick · Provincial

Creates a new job-protected leave of up to 27 weeks of UNPAID leave in any 52-week period for an employee's own serious illness or injury, available to employees with more than 90 continuous days of service, with a duty to notify the employer promptly. It is a large increase from the previous five-day allowance and harmonizes N.B. with most other provinces and federal EI sickness-benefit timelines.

✅ The case for

Protects seriously ill or injured workers from losing their jobs while they recover; aligns N.B. with the rest of Canada and with EI sickness benefits; a law professor said it brings N.B. 'in line with the rest of Canada.' Strongly supported by labour and health advocates.

⚠️ The case against

The leave is UNPAID, so lower-income workers may not be able to afford to take it; advocates wanted paid sick days (business groups pushed back on paid leave in a 2023 consultation). Employers, especially small businesses, cite staffing and cost-of-coverage concerns over a 27-week absence.

🔐 Privacy & civil liberties: Minimal. Employers may request reasonable medical documentation; standard employment-standards handling of health information applies.

👥 Who it affects

Employees who suffer serious illness/injury; employers (especially small businesses managing coverage); the broader N.B. labour force.

✔️ Fact-checkBill 26, An Act to Amend the Employment Standards Act (long-term illness and injury leave), received Royal Assent on June 12, 2026; it creates a new job-protected, unpaid Long-Term Illness and Injury Leave of up to 27 weeks in any 52-week period for employees with more than 90 continuous days of service, supported by a medical certificate. The leave is unpaid (not paid sick leave) and is separate from, and does not replace, an employer's human-rights duty to accommodate; it brings New Brunswick roughly into line with the longer illness-leave entitlements in most other provinces and the federal jurisdiction.
