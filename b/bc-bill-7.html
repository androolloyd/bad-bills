Bill 7LOWER CONCERN

Post-Secondary International Education (Designated Institutions) Act

Sponsor: Government (Ministry of Post-Secondary Education and Future Skills) · Status: Introduced February 19, 2026; passed/advanced through the spring sitting (third reading by late May 2026). Confirm Royal Assent date if precision is needed. · British Columbia · Provincial

Puts BC's Education Quality Assurance (EQA) designation into statute and makes it a legal requirement for any public or private institution to host international students on federal study permits. Sets quality criteria, gives government inspection and enforcement powers, and prohibits non-designated institutions from advertising, enrolling, or delivering study-permit programs. Responds to abuse by low-quality 'diploma mill' private colleges amid the federal study-permit cap.

✅ The case for

Protects international students from exploitative operators, upholds BC's education reputation, and adds real enforcement teeth where rules were previously just policy. Broadly welcomed as consumer/student protection in a sector worth ~$4 billion to BC.

⚠️ The case against

Some private-sector and sector observers worry about compliance burden and government discretion over designations, and about downstream effects on enrolment and institutional viability under the federal cap. Largely seen as reasonable oversight rather than rights-infringing.

🔐 Privacy & civil liberties: Minimal. Inspection/enforcement powers involve institutional (not primarily personal) data; limited individual-privacy footprint.

👥 Who it affects

International students; public and private post-secondary institutions hosting them; the education-export sector.

✔️ Fact-checkVerified: Bill 7 (Post-Secondary International Education (Designated Institutions) Act), introduced Feb. 19, 2026 by Post-Secondary Education and Future Skills Minister Jessie Sunner, puts B.C.'s Education Quality Assurance (EQA) designation into statute, makes designation a legal requirement to host international students on federal study permits, prohibits non-designated institutions from advertising/enrolling/delivering such programs, and creates inspection and enforcement powers. No notable misinformation found; the act regulates which institutions may host study-permit international students and does not, by itself, set provincial study-permit caps (those are set federally by IRCC).
