Bill 29HIGH CONCERN

Health Statutes Amendment Act, 2026

Sponsor: Minister of Health (UCP) · Status: Passed and received Royal Assent in the spring sitting; key provisions take effect later in 2026 with details in regulations (expected by fall 2026). Some provisions align terminology with not-yet-proclaimed 'dual practice' amendments from the prior Bill 11. · Alberta · Provincial

Amends the Alberta Health Care Insurance Act to let Albertans access certain private-pay preventative/diagnostic tests without a provider referral, and amends the Pharmacy and Drug Act to let authorized prescribers keep limited supplies of certain addiction-treatment medications for urgent situations. It is the latest step in Alberta's multi-year 'refocusing' and its move toward a dual-practice (mixed public-private) model.

✅ The case for

Government and supporters argue it expands access, cuts wait times, lets patients pay for preventative screening they want, and adds capacity through private delivery; the addiction-medication change can speed urgent treatment.

⚠️ The case against

Health-policy critics (CCPA, the Tyee, health coalitions, legal experts on the related Bill 11) warn that allowing physicians to bill private patients for faster service entrenches two-tier care, contravenes Canada Health Act principles of accessibility/universality, can lengthen public-system waits, and lets income determine speed of care - potentially pressuring patients to pay or 'go to the back of the line.'

🔐 Privacy & civil liberties: Expanding private-pay testing and dual-practice billing raises questions about handling of health data by private providers and insurers and about equity of access. No new surveillance regime, but private-sector health data flows merit oversight. Privacy: low-to-moderate; equity/rights-to-care concern: high.

👥 Who it affects

All Albertans relying on the public health system, lower-income patients (risk of unequal access), patients wanting private screening, people in addiction treatment, physicians, and the federal-provincial Canada Health Act transfer relationship.

✔️ Fact-checkVerified: Bill 29, the Health Statutes Amendment Act, 2026, was introduced April 14, 2026 by Primary and Preventative Health Services Minister Adriana LaGrange and has passed third reading; it amends the Alberta Health Care Insurance Act to lay groundwork for accessing certain privately paid preventative diagnostic tests without a provider referral, and amends the Pharmacy and Drug Act to let authorized prescribers keep a limited supply of certain addiction-treatment medications for urgent use. Common misinformation: the bill does not itself list which tests will be self-referable or privatize the public system — those specifics are deferred to regulations expected in fall 2026; critics frame it as enabling two-tier care, while the government frames it as expanding access.
