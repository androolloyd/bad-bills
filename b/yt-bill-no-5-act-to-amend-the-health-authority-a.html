Act to amend the Health Authority Act (2026)

Sponsor: Brad Cathers, Minister of Health and Social Services (Yukon Party) · Status: Assented to. Tabled March 23, 2026; passed third reading March 31, 2026 by a 10-6 vote (Yukon Party for; NDP and Liberals against). In a notable partial reversal, at the May 8, 2026 Yukon Forum in Teslin, Premier Dixon committed to First Nations leaders NOT to fully repeal the Health Authority Act or make further amendments in the fall sitting, pledging to reassess the path forward jointly. All seven members of the interim health authority board (Shaw Kwa'a) had resigned April 27, 2026 after their mandate and funding were stripped. · Yukon · Provincial

The amendment gives the Health Minister legal authority to pause implementation of Yukon's planned territorial health authority (Shaw Kwa'a). It empowers the minister to stop transition work and override the interim board, and adds a clause shielding the government and board members from civil lawsuits arising from the pause. The government says pausing lets it redirect resources to frontline care, recruitment and hospitals; the original Health Authority Act was co-developed with Yukon First Nations.

✅ The case for

Government argues the pause lets it redirect money to frontline services, health-worker recruitment and hospital facilities rather than a costly governance transition, and cites unresolved issues such as pension impacts for health-care workers. Provides legal clarity for halting the transition. The minister said the civil-immunity clause was added partly in response to concerns raised by the interim board chair.

⚠️ The case against

Yukon First Nations, the Council of Yukon First Nations and the Chiefs Committee on Health strongly opposed the move as unilateral, saying it disregards years of co-development and damages co-governance commitments under self-government agreements. NDP Leader Kate White called the amendments unilateral and damaging to First Nations relationships. Critics (and CBC) called the initial tabling 'sneaky.' The entire interim board resigned in protest. Opposition MLAs questioned why the legal shield covers the government and interim board but not the Yukon Hospital Corporation, alleging accountability gaps.

🔐 Privacy & civil liberties: Minimal on data/surveillance. The notable civil-liberties feature is the immunity clause barring civil causes of action against the government and board members related to the pause, which narrows affected parties' access to legal remedies/redress - a due-process concern rather than a privacy one.

👥 Who it affects

All Yukoners who use the health system; Yukon First Nations (whose self-government and co-development rights are engaged); health-care workers; the interim health authority board; and the Yukon Hospital Corporation.
