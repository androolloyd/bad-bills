Bills 46 & 47RIGHTS-POSITIVE

Energy Sector Consumer Advocate Act + An Act Respecting the Energy Sector Consumer Advocate (with related An Act to Amend the Electricity Act)

Sponsor: Hon. René Legacy (Deputy Premier / Minister of Finance and Treasury Board / Energy file; Liberal) · Status: Royal Assent / law (passed spring 2026; tabled late May 2026). Verified on legnb.ca. · New Brunswick · Provincial

Creates a new Energy Sector Consumer Advocate to represent residential customers and small businesses before the Energy and Utilities Board (EUB). The advocate can investigate complaints from individual NB Power customers and refer unresolved issues to the EUB. It replaces the existing 'public intervener' role. This follows an independent review of NB Power (the public utility's rising rates, aging infrastructure and roughly $6-billion debt).

✅ The case for

Gives ordinary ratepayers and small businesses a dedicated champion at the regulator and a complaints-handling avenue against a monopoly utility; a former intervener called the bill 'quite progressive'; responds directly to public anger over electricity rate hikes; part of a broader NB Power accountability push.

⚠️ The case against

Replacing the 'public intervener' (who represented the broad public interest in rate cases) with a complaints/consumer-focused advocate could narrow who speaks for the overall public interest in big rate decisions. Critics/Global News noted the government did not fully commit to non-interference / arms-length independence for the utility, leaving questions about political influence.

🔐 Privacy & civil liberties: Minimal. Handles customer billing/service complaints; no new surveillance or data powers beyond what is needed to investigate complaints.

👥 Who it affects

NB Power residential customers and small businesses; the EUB; NB Power; the former public intervener; all electricity ratepayers.

✔️ Fact-checkBill 46 (Energy Sector Consumer Advocate Act) and Bill 47 (An Act Respecting the Energy Sector Consumer Advocate), both sponsored by Energy Minister Hon. René Legacy, received Royal Assent on June 12, 2026; together they create an Energy Sector Consumer Advocate to investigate complaints and represent residents and small businesses before the Energy and Utilities Board, replacing the existing public-intervener role, with the office expected to begin operating in fiscal 2027-2028. The package stems from the NB Power review (government accepted 44 of 50 recommendations); a frequent misconception is that it sets or freezes electricity rates, but the advocate cannot set rates, and the government has not committed to barring future rate intervention.
