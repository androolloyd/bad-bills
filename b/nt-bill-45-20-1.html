Bill 45-20(1)HIGH CONCERN

Civil Forfeiture Act

Sponsor: Hon. Jay Macdonald, Minister of Justice (Government bill) · Status: Introduced and given first and second reading March 4-5, 2026; referred to the Standing Committee on Social Development. Committee/clause-by-clause review under way; awaiting committee report, third reading and final vote (not yet passed as of mid-June 2026). · Northwest Territories · Provincial

Civil forfeiture lets the government apply to a civil court to seize property (cash, vehicles, real estate) that it alleges is the proceeds or an instrument of unlawful activity — without any criminal charge or conviction. The standard of proof is the civil 'balance of probabilities' rather than the criminal 'beyond a reasonable doubt.' The GNWT frames it as a tool to 'take the profit out of crime' amid a drug-trafficking crisis. Similar regimes exist in most provinces. MLAs flagged that the bill can reach back to alleged conduct predating the law and lacks a minimum-offence threshold.

✅ The case for

Aims to disrupt organized crime and drug trafficking by removing financial incentives; responds to urgent community alarm about drugs and violence (e.g., Inuvik residents calling it 'a state of emergency'); allows action where criminal prosecution is difficult; mirrors established legislation in most other provinces.

⚠️ The case against

Reverses the usual burden and lowers the standard of proof, so people can lose property without being convicted of (or even charged with) a crime; raises due-process and presumption-of-innocence concerns; retroactive 'reach-back' to old conduct is contentious (and harder to prove, per officials); no minimum-offence threshold means even minor matters could trigger seizure; civil-rights groups note similar laws elsewhere have faced constitutional challenge; risk of disproportionate impact on poor and Indigenous people who often cannot afford to contest a forfeiture.

🔐 Privacy & civil liberties: Medium. Civil-forfeiture investigations and asset-tracing involve gathering and sharing detailed financial, property and personal records, often without the procedural protections of a criminal trial, and can affect family members who co-own property. Concerns center on due process and the protection of property as much as on data privacy.

👥 Who it affects

People (and their relatives/co-owners) whose property is alleged to be tied to unlawful activity; alleged drug traffickers and organized crime; the courts and the Department of Justice; potentially anyone whose vehicle, cash or home is linked to an offence.

✔️ Fact-checkVerified: Bill 45 would allow seizure of crime-linked property through civil court on the balance of probabilities without a criminal conviction; it received first/second reading March 4-5, 2026 and was referred to committee, where MLAs questioned its retroactive 'reach-back' and lack of a minimum-offence threshold — it had not yet passed third reading as of mid-June 2026.
