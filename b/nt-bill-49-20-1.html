Bill 49-20(1)HIGH CONCERN

Safer Communities and Neighbourhoods Act (SCAN)

Sponsor: Hon. Jay Macdonald, Minister of Justice (Government bill) · Status: Introduced June 1, 2026; first reading June 1, second reading June 2, 2026; Standing Committee amendments completed June 3, 2026. Awaiting Committee of the Whole, third reading and Assent (not yet passed as of mid-June 2026). · Northwest Territories · Provincial

SCAN creates a civil enforcement process letting residents confidentially report properties 'habitually used' for illegal activity (e.g., drug trafficking, illegal alcohol sales). A SCAN director/investigators can investigate complaints — or act on evidence of ongoing illegal activity even without a complaint or landlord involvement — and ultimately seek to shut down or evict from problem properties through civil orders rather than criminal prosecution. Investigators are required to work with Indigenous governments, social services and housing organizations, and occupants may be given a chance to change behaviour before further action. SCAN laws exist in several provinces; the NWT version is modelled on them.

✅ The case for

Gives communities and enforcement agencies a tool to address chronic 'problem properties,' organized crime and bootlegging that residents say are driving fear and violence; confidential complaints may protect reporters from retaliation; the bill builds in collaboration with Indigenous governments, social services and housing providers and an opportunity for occupants to reform before eviction; the minister stresses it targets illegal operations, not people struggling with addiction, poverty, homelessness or mental health.

⚠️ The case against

A civil eviction process with a lower burden of proof can sidestep tenant protections; a Yukon lawyer and civil-liberties advocates warn that parts of similar SCAN/eviction laws have been found unconstitutional and that the NWT bill could face Charter challenge; social-service organizations strongly opposed it, warning evictions can render dependents (children, seniors) homeless and simply displace crime; the director's power to act without a complaint or landlord raises due-process and oversight concerns; some residents call it potentially redundant with existing tenancy, housing and police processes.

🔐 Privacy & civil liberties: High. SCAN relies on confidential third-party reporting and investigative surveillance of households suspected of illegal activity, with information shared among a director, RCMP, Indigenous governments, social services and housing bodies. Anonymous complaints can be hard to test or rebut, and targets — often vulnerable tenants — may have limited ability to see or contest the evidence used to evict them.

👥 Who it affects

Tenants and homeowners of properties subject to complaints (and their household dependents); landlords and housing authorities; people involved in drug trafficking/bootlegging; Indigenous governments and social-service agencies; neighbours affected by problem properties.
