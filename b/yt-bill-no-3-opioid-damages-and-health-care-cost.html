Opioid Damages and Health Care Costs Recovery Act, 2026

Sponsor: Brad Cathers, Minister of Health and Social Services (Yukon Party) · Status: Assented to. Tabled late March 2026; passed by unanimous vote of the Legislative Assembly on April 2, 2026; received assent April 7, 2026. · Yukon · Provincial

Creates the legal framework for Yukon to join the British Columbia-led national class action against manufacturers, wholesalers and consultants of opioid products to recover public health-care costs of the opioid crisis (emergency response, hospital care, long-term supports). Modelled on B.C.'s 2018 Act (upheld 6-1 by the Supreme Court of Canada), it lets Yukon prove harm using aggregate, statistical and population-level data rather than individual patient records, and includes litigation advantages such as a reverse onus on defendants, suspended/extended limitation periods, restricted discovery and retroactive application. The B.C.-led suit is set for trial in February 2028.

✅ The case for

Lets a small territory recover a share of opioid-crisis costs efficiently by joining an existing national class action without onerous documentary discovery, sharing litigation costs and risk. Using population-based/statistical evidence avoids forcing disclosure of individual patients' identities or records. Passed unanimously - rare all-party agreement. Backed by SCC-affirmed constitutional precedent. Targets industry actors widely blamed for fueling the crisis.

⚠️ The case against

From a defendant/rule-of-law perspective the model is procedurally aggressive: reverse onuses shift the burden of proof onto defendants, limitation periods are suspended for years, discovery rights are restricted, and the law applies retroactively to past conduct - features critics of such statutes elsewhere argue strain ordinary civil-procedure fairness. Actual recovery depends on the outcome of the multi-year B.C.-led litigation, so any financial benefit to Yukon is uncertain and distant (trial in 2028).

🔐 Privacy & civil liberties: Positive/protective on individual privacy: the Act expressly allows the government to use statistical and population-based information as evidence and does NOT require it to identify specific individuals who received care or to produce their health records; identifying information must be stripped from any disclosed documents. The civil-liberties tension is not privacy but defendants' procedural rights (reverse onus, retroactivity, curtailed discovery).

👥 Who it affects

Opioid manufacturers, distributors and consultants (defendants); the Yukon public treasury/health system (potential cost recovery); and, indirectly, Yukoners affected by the opioid crisis. Individual patients' privacy is protected by design.
