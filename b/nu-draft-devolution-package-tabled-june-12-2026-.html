Draft devolution package (tabled June 12, 2026; NOT yet introduced)WATCH CLOSELY

Public Lands Act; Waters Act; Surface Rights Tribunal Act; Archaeological and Palaeontological Sites Act (foundational devolution legislation)

Sponsor: Premier John Main (Executive and Intergovernmental Affairs); future bills via the new Department of Mines, Natural Resources and Land (MNRL) · Status: DRAFT / NOT YET INTRODUCED - Premier Main released draft versions of these four foundational bills for public review on June 12, 2026, an unusual pre-first-reading step. They are expected to be formally introduced in the fall 2026 and winter 2027 sittings, ahead of devolution taking effect April 1, 2027. Two further bills (Petroleum Resources Act, Oil and Gas Operations Act) remain in development. · Nunavut · Provincial

These are the most consequential pieces of legislation on Nunavut's horizon. Under the Nunavut Lands and Resources Devolution Agreement (signed Jan 18, 2024 by Canada, the GN and Nunavut Tunngavik Inc.), control over public lands, freshwater and onshore oil/gas/mineral resources transfers from Ottawa to Nunavut on April 1, 2027. Nunavut must enact territorial laws that substantially 'mirror' the corresponding federal Acts before that date. The Public Lands Act, Waters Act, Surface Rights Tribunal Act and Archaeological and Palaeontological Sites Act establish the legal framework for the GN to administer lands, water licensing, surface-access dispute resolution, and heritage-site protection. A new Department of Mines, Natural Resources and Land (MNRL) will administer these powers, including a Nunavut Geological Survey. Released early as drafts so MLAs and the public can review them before formal introduction.

✅ The case for

Devolution is a historic transfer of self-government and resource authority to a majority-Inuit territory - a major advance in Indigenous self-determination and local control over lands and waters; releasing drafts early for public/MLA review is unusually transparent and supports informed debate; the Surface Rights Tribunal Act provides a mechanism to balance landholder and developer interests; the Archaeological and Palaeontological Sites Act can strengthen protection of Inuit heritage and cultural sites.

⚠️ The case against

Because the bills must 'mirror' federal law, critics question how much genuine policy latitude Nunavut will have at the outset; tight timeline (all foundational bills must pass before April 1, 2027) raises concern about adequate consultation and the GN's administrative capacity to assume these complex responsibilities; interaction with Nunavut Tunngavik Inc., Inuit-owned lands and the Nunavut Agreement (land claim) creates jurisdictional complexity; resource-development provisions could open Nunavut to expanded mining and oil/gas activity with environmental and Inuit-rights implications.

🔐 Privacy & civil liberties: Minimal as drafted. These are lands/resources/heritage administration statutes, not surveillance laws. Possible incidental civil-liberties dimensions: surface-rights and water-licensing regimes involve property/access rights and dispute adjudication, and heritage-site rules may regulate land access; any compelled-information or enforcement/inspection powers in the final texts should be reviewed for due-process and Inuit consultation safeguards. No personal-data surveillance powers identified in the draft framework.

👥 Who it affects

All Nunavummiut (especially Inuit, ~85% of the population); Nunavut Tunngavik Inc. and regional Inuit associations; the mining and oil/gas sector; land users and harvesters; the new MNRL department; future GN fiscal capacity via resource revenues.
