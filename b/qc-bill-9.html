Bill 9LOWER CONCERN

Act to prevent the harmful effects of energy drinks on youth health

Status: Passed — adopted 11 June 2026 and sanctioned. · Quebec · Provincial

Restricts the sale of caffeinated 'energy drinks' to people under 16 (and tightens marketing/availability to youth) as a public-health measure.

✅ The case for

Public-health rationale: limits youth exposure to high-caffeine products; popular protective measure for minors.

⚠️ The case against

Critics question enforceability and whether age limits are the right tool versus broader regulation/education; minor paternalism debate and retail-compliance costs.

🔐 Privacy & civil liberties: Minimal — possible age-verification at point of sale is the only data-touch point.

👥 Who it affects

Minors under 16, parents, retailers, and beverage manufacturers.

✔️ Fact-checkVerified: Bill 9 (An Act to prevent the harmful effects of energy drinks on the health of young people) was adopted June 10-11, 2026 (passed 103-1, assent June 11), making Quebec the first jurisdiction in North America to restrict caffeinated energy drinks to people under 16. It bans sale to minors under 16 (including online sales and vending machines), imposes in-person sales requirements, and creates an inspection/penalty regime; an 'energy drink' is defined as a beverage with at least 150 mg caffeine per litre plus ingredients like taurine/vitamins, expressly excluding coffee, tea and certain natural health products. It takes effect about six months after assent. Correction: it restricts sales to youth and does not ban energy drinks outright; it was spurred partly by the 2024 death of 15-year-old Zachary Miron.
